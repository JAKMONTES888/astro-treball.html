What is being investigated

A clock spinning on a dead star.

A pulsar is a rapidly rotating neutron star whose lighthouse-like beam of radio emission, sweeping past Earth, produces astonishingly stable periodic pulses. When such a pulsar orbits another compact body, the timing of those pulses becomes a direct probe of gravity.

The central problem is conceptually simple: General Relativity describes how mass curves spacetime, but for over half a century its predictions could only be tested in the slow, weak-field arena of the Solar System. Then, in the summer of 1974, Hulse and Taylor discovered the first binary pulsar — and an entirely new regime of experimental gravity opened up.

Binary pulsars allow physicists to study two strongly self-gravitating bodies — neutron stars whose internal gravitational binding energy is comparable to their rest-mass energy — orbiting each other in spacetimes very different from Minkowski. Pulse arrival times are recorded with microsecond precision, and from them one extracts a small set of "post-Keplerian" parameters that quantify relativistic deviations from Newtonian motion.

Each measured post-Keplerian parameter draws a curve in a mass–mass diagram. If the theory of gravity is correct, every curve must intersect at one common pair of masses. General Relativity has passed every such test ever performed, in some cases at the level of a few parts in a hundred thousand.

Pulsar laboratories also constrain alternative theories that predict effects absent in GR — dipolar gravitational waves, violations of the universality of free fall for self-gravitating bodies, time variation of Newton's constant, preferred-frame and preferred-location effects. So far, every attempted falsification has confirmed Einstein's theory.

+

Lighthouse model

A misaligned magnetic axis sweeps a beam of coherent radio emission past Earth once per rotation, producing the characteristic pulses.

The pulsar is a magnetised neutron star whose magnetic axis is generally misaligned with its rotation axis. Radio emission emerges anisotropically from regions near the open magnetic field lines around the magnetic poles. Distant observers detect emission only during the fraction of each rotation when part of the beam points toward Earth.

Critically, the position angle of linear polarisation changes with spin phase in a way consistent with the changing orientation of the magnetic field lines along the line of sight — the empirical confirmation of the model. From these polarisation sweeps the rotation geometry is determined: the angle between the magnetic axis and the spin axis, and between the spin axis and our line of sight.

Despite the geometric model being firmly established since 1968, the underlying mechanism producing the coherent radio emission remains poorly understood even today — but this gap does not impede any of the gravity experiments described elsewhere in this review.

+

Folding & timing

Thousands of noisy individual pulses are averaged into a stable profile; comparison with a template yields a Time of Arrival to fractions of a microsecond.

Individual pulses vary chaotically in shape and amplitude — by themselves they are not useful. But adding them in phase produces an extraordinarily stable average profile, characteristic of each pulsar. This process is called folding.

To fold properly, an a priori estimate of the spin frequency at the receiving telescope is needed. This local frequency changes constantly because of the time conversion between pulsar proper time and observatory coordinate time. The prediction is made by a timing program using the best available pulsar ephemeris.

A high signal-to-noise version of the folded profile becomes the template. Cross-correlating each new observation with the template produces the Time of Arrival (ToA), the fundamental observable. For PSR J1909−3744 the precision reaches 0.1 µs — though such precision is rare, limited by signal-to-noise, dispersion variations, and intrinsic pulse jitter.

+

Keplerian orbit

Five parameters (P_b, x, e, ω, T_0) describe the Newtonian binary motion encoded in the modulation of the ToAs.

The five Keplerian parameters describing the radial orbital motion of the pulsar are: orbital period P_b, projected semi-major axis of the pulsar's orbit x = a_p sin i / c, eccentricity e, longitude of periastron ω, and time of periastron passage T_0.

From these, the mass function follows directly:

f(M_p, M_c, i) = (M_c sin i)³ / (M_p + M_c)² = x³ / T_⊙ · (P_b/2π)⁻²

Two further parameters — the inclination i and the position angle of the ascending node Ω — generally cannot be determined from radial-motion measurements alone. Pulsar timing is fundamentally one-dimensional: only the line-of-sight component of orbital motion is accessible.

For ~80% of binary pulsars, the mass function above is the only available mass constraint. For the rest, post-Keplerian parameters or optical observations of the companion break the degeneracy.

+

Post-Keplerian PK

Small relativistic corrections — periastron advance, Einstein delay, Shapiro delay, orbital decay, geometric deformations — each measurable as a separate parameter.

Post-Keplerian parameters quantify relativistic deviations from Keplerian motion. They are theory-independent phenomenological observables — fitted from timing data without assuming any specific gravity theory. The standard set:

  • ω̇ · time-averaged advance of periastron
  • γ · amplitude of the Einstein delay (gravitational redshift + transverse Doppler)
  • Ṗ_b · rate of change of orbital period — dominated by GW damping in compact systems
  • r, s · range and shape of the Shapiro delay
  • δ_θ, δ_r · relativistic angular and radial deformations of the orbit

In GR, each PK parameter is a simple function of the Keplerian parameters and the two component masses. This "PK simplicity" is a consequence of the effacement of internal structure: in GR, the motion of bodies in an N-body system is independent of their internal composition. Most alternative theories break this property.

+

Mass–mass diagram

Each PK observable is a curve in the (m_p, m_c) plane. GR demands all curves cross at one point.

For every measured PK parameter, GR predicts a relation between the two component masses. Plotting that relation as a curve in the m_p versus m_c plane produces one constraint. Two PK parameters give two curves whose intersection determines both masses. Three or more PK parameters give a self-consistency test: all curves must intersect at one common point.

If they do, GR has passed the test. If they don't, GR is in trouble. So far it has never been in trouble.

The Hulse–Taylor pulsar offered three crossing curves; the Double Pulsar today offers six. The Double Pulsar's mass–mass diagram is the most stringent quasi-stationary + radiative test of GR ever performed in the strong-field regime, with all curves agreeing at relative precision ~6×10⁻⁵.

+

Strong Equivalence Principle

The architecture of GR conjectured to be unique among metric theories that fully embody the SEP — the property pulsars test most stringently.

The SEP is an extension of the Einstein Equivalence Principle to include local gravitational experiments with self-gravitating bodies. It encompasses three statements:

  • Universality of free fall extends to self-gravitating bodies in an external gravitational field — no Nordtvedt effect
  • No preferred-location effects — the outcome of any local experiment is independent of where and when in the universe
  • No preferred-frame effects — independent of the motion of the local freely-falling reference frame

It is conjectured that GR is the only viable metric theory of gravity that fully embodies the SEP. Pulsar tests are uniquely powerful for SEP because neutron stars carry significant fractional gravitational binding energy (~10–20% of rest-mass), unlike Solar-System bodies (≲10⁻⁶). Any SEP violation hidden by smallness in the Solar System becomes amplified in pulsar systems.

How we measure

The grammar of pulse arrival times.

Behind every gravity test lies the same workflow: capture a faint, broadband, polarised radio signal; correct it for the dispersive interstellar medium; fold thousands of pulses into a stable profile; and compare every measured arrival time against a relativistic timing model.

I
Detection

Single dishes capture broadband voltages

Pulsars are faint, point-like radio sources, brightest at decimetric wavelengths. Sensitivity matters above all — which is why single-dish telescopes (FAST, Effelsberg, Green Bank, Parkes/Murriyang, Nançay, the former Arecibo) and large arrays operating in tied-array mode (MeerKAT, uGMRT) dominate the field.

Two voltage streams — one per polarisation — are sampled at gigasamples per second and channelised by a digital filterbank, producing four Stokes-parameter spectra (I, Q, U, V) every few tens of microseconds.

II
Dedispersion

The interstellar plasma delays low frequencies

Free electrons along the line of sight slow lower-frequency radio waves, smearing pulses in time. The delay scales as Δt ∝ DM/F², where DM is the dispersion measure (column density of free electrons).

Δt(F) = 4.1488 · DM / F² [ms, with F in GHz]

Coherent dedispersion — applied in the Fourier domain to the raw voltages — completely removes the smearing within each channel and is the standard for high-precision timing today.

III
Folding

Thousands of noisy pulses become one stable profile

Individual pulses vary chaotically in shape and amplitude; their average is extraordinarily stable and characteristic of each pulsar. Folding — adding pulses in phase using an a priori spin model — produces this template.

The Time of Arrival (ToA) is then obtained by cross-correlating the observed profile with the template. Modern recycled millisecond pulsars deliver ToAs precise to a fraction of a microsecond.

IV
Timing model

Every relativistic effect adds a delay

The measured ToA is referenced back to the proper time of the pulsar by accounting for, in succession: the Einstein delay (special-relativistic time dilation and Solar-System gravitational redshift), the Rømer delay (geometric light propagation), the Shapiro delay (curvature-induced delay near the Sun and the binary companion), the dispersive delay, and — for binary pulsars — the four orbital delays D_R, D_S, D_E, D_A.

Programs such as TEMPO, TEMPO2 and PINT minimise the χ² of normalised residuals, fitting astrometric, spin, dispersion and orbital parameters simultaneously. A phase-coherent solution accounts unambiguously for every rotation between any two ToAs.

V
Post-Keplerian fit

Five Keplerian + post-Keplerian observables

The DD86 model parametrises orbital relativity through observables that, in GR, are simple functions of the Keplerian parameters and the two component masses:

ω̇ = 3·(Pb/2π)⁻⁵ᐟ³ · (T⊙ M)²ᐟ³ · (1−e²)⁻¹ · advance of periastron
γ = (Pb/2π)¹ᐟ³ · (T⊙ M)²ᐟ³ · X_c(1+X_c) · e · Einstein delay
Ṗ_b = −(192π/5) · (Pb/2π)⁻⁵ᐟ³ · (T⊙M)⁵ᐟ³ · X_p X_c · f(e) · GW damping
r = T⊙ M_c ; s = sin i · Shapiro range & shape

Each measurement gives a curve in the (m_p, m_c) plane. The intersection of all curves at a single point is the consistency test that GR has never failed.

Pulsar timing arrays

The stochastic gravitational-wave background.

Pulsars are not only laboratories for binary gravity. Networks of millisecond pulsars, timed to nanosecond precision over decades, act as galactic-scale gravitational-wave detectors.

When a low-frequency gravitational wave passes between Earth and a distant pulsar, it imprints a tiny, correlated perturbation on the pulse arrival times. By cross-correlating timing residuals from many pulsars across the sky, Pulsar Timing Arrays — NANOGrav, EPTA, PPTA, InPTA, MPTA — search for the diffuse stochastic gravitational-wave background: the sum of every supermassive black-hole binary in the universe spiraling toward merger.

In 2023, three independent collaborations announced evidence for this signal. Pulsar timing — the same technique that confirmed gravitational waves indirectly fifty years ago through the Hulse–Taylor binary — has now begun to detect them directly.

Why this matters

A field that won't stop testing.

Why does it matter that, fifty years after the discovery of the first binary pulsar, General Relativity still wins every contest? It matters because the universe could have been otherwise — and might still be, in regions we have not yet reached.

Solar System tests of gravity probe the post-Newtonian limit, where curvature is feeble and velocities are tiny. Pulsars probe an entirely different regime: two compact bodies whose gravitational binding energies are a sizeable fraction of their rest-mass energies, orbiting in spacetimes whose curvature exceeds anything imaged by the Event Horizon Telescope. That GR survives here is not a foregone conclusion. Many alternative theories — Bekenstein's TeVeS, large parts of Damour–Esposito-Farèse gravity, certain Einstein-Aether and scalar-Gauss-Bonnet models — predict observable departures: dipolar gravitational waves, scalarised neutron stars, a time-varying gravitational constant, preferred-frame effects that polarise circular orbits. None has been seen.

The pulsar laboratory therefore plays a complementary role to the LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA network and to event-horizon-scale imaging. Each tests gravity in a distinct regime; together they triangulate the empirical landscape of strong-field physics. And pulsar tests have a unique virtue — they are quasi-stationary: every effect accumulates over decades of observation, and the precision grows with time.

What remains? The detection of a pulsar–black-hole binary; a measurement of the moment of inertia of a neutron star to constrain the dense-matter equation of state; the discovery of more compact double-neutron-star systems where Lense–Thirring precession and second-post-Newtonian effects become routinely measurable; a relativistic pulsar in orbit around the supermassive black hole at the Galactic Centre. Each of these is plausible within the next decade. Each could rewrite chapters of this review.
